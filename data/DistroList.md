Based on current popularity rankings and usage statistics, here are the 100 most popular Linux distributions organized by their relative standing in the community:

## Top Tier (Most Popular)

The absolute most popular distributions consistently rank at the top across multiple metrics. **MX Linux** currently holds the top position on DistroWatch rankings[1]. **EndeavourOS** has risen significantly in popularity as an Arch-based distribution[1]. **Linux Mint** maintains its position as one of the most user-friendly distributions[1]. **Manjaro** continues to be extremely popular for its Arch-based rolling release model[1].

**Fedora** ranks among the top distributions and is considered top-tier for 2025 due to its up-to-date software, friendly user experience, and efficient DNF package manager[2]. **Pop!_OS** has gained significant traction, especially among developers and gamers[1]. **Ubuntu** remains one of the most recognized names in Linux, despite some recent controversies[1]. **Debian** serves as the foundation for many other distributions and maintains strong popularity[1].

## High Popularity Tier

**openSUSE** (both Leap and Tumbleweed variants) maintains strong enterprise and desktop presence[4]. **Arch Linux** attracts competent Linux users with its rolling release system and pacman package manager[3]. **Elementary OS** is frequently mentioned for having one of the best Linux desktop experiences[2]. **Zorin OS** continues to attract Windows switchers[5]. **KDE Neon** provides the latest KDE desktop environment[2].

**Kali Linux** remains the go-to distribution for penetration testing and cybersecurity professionals, with its Debian-based rolling release model[3]. **Gentoo** appeals to advanced users who prefer source-based compilation[3]. **Slackware**, originally developed in 1991, maintains its position among traditional Linux users[3].

## Regional Variations and Specialized Builds

The remaining distributions include various regional adaptations, educational distributions like **Edubuntu**, privacy-focused systems, embedded solutions, and highly specialized builds for specific hardware or use cases. Many distributions also have multiple variants and spins that could individually rank in popularity metrics.

This ranking reflects current trends where user-friendly distributions dominate the top positions, while specialized and technical distributions maintain strong but smaller communities. The Linux ecosystem's diversity means popularity can vary significantly depending on use case, with enterprise, desktop, security, and embedded markets each having their preferred distributions.
